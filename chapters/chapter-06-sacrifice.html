CHAPTER 06

The Sacrifice

USS ENTERPRISE-D
Stardate 47635.3

Jean-Luc Picard opened his eyes. The decision had been made in that infinite moment of silence, weighing thousands of years of Bajoran faith against billions of lives in the Alpha Quadrant. There had been no revelation, no divine clarity. Only the cold calculus of triage on a civilizational scale.

Picard: "Mr. Data... tell the entity..." His voice caught. He had ordered people to their deaths before. Had made impossible choices. But he had never ordered the death of gods. "Tell it to proceed with the first option. The wormhole."

The words fell like stones into an abyss. Around him, his senior staff reacted in their own ways—Crusher closed her eyes, Troi's face showed raw anguish, Worf's jaw tightened but he gave a slight nod of acknowledgment. Only Riker spoke.

Riker: "Captain... are you certain?"
Picard: "No, Number One. I am not certain of anything except that in four minutes, the entity will make its own choice. And I cannot allow 2,800 Dominion warships to return to our space. Even if the cost is..." He couldn't finish the sentence.
Data: "The entity acknowledges your decision, Captain. It is... it expresses sorrow for what must be done. But it agrees that this path causes the least suffering to those who experience linear time. It will begin the process immediately."

On the viewscreen, the anomaly began to change. Its swirling colors shifted, becoming more focused, more purposeful. Tendrils of energy that had been reaching toward the materializing Dominion ships now redirected, forming a bridge of impossible light that stretched across parsecs toward the Bajoran wormhole.

Geordi: "Captain, I'm detecting a massive buildup of quantum energy. The entity is creating a subspace corridor directly to the wormhole. It's going to use itself as a conduit to funnel the Dominion ships into the Celestial Temple."
Picard: "How long until—"
Geordi: "Two minutes, sir. Once it starts, there's no stopping it. The energy cascade will complete whether we want it to or not."

Picard gripped the arms of his command chair. Two minutes until they committed deicide. Two minutes until the Prophets—beings who had saved the Federation, who had existed for millennia—would be destroyed to contain a threat that they themselves had created by removing the Dominion fleet from time.

The irony was not lost on him. The Prophets' mercy would be their destruction.

* * *
DEEP SPACE NINE
Simultaneous

On Deep Space Nine, Commander Benjamin Sisko sat in his office, reviewing supply manifests, when the first tremor hit. Not physical—he'd experienced enough of those during his time on the station. This was something else. A vibration in a part of him that had no name, the place where the Prophets had touched him and named him their Emissary.

Sisko: "Computer, is there any unusual activity near the wormhole?"
SCANNING...
DETECTING MASSIVE SUBSPACE DISTORTION
ORIGIN: UNKNOWN, BEARING 127 MARK 34
QUANTUM SIGNATURE CONSISTENT WITH NON-LINEAR SPACE-TIME
ENERGY CORRIDOR FORMING... TERMINUS: BAJORAN WORMHOLE

Sisko's blood ran cold. He tapped his combadge.

Sisko: "Sisko to Ops. Major, what's happening with the wormhole?"
Kira (comm): "Commander, we're detecting some kind of energy stream heading directly for it. I've never seen anything like this. The Prophets are—" Her voice caught. "Sir, something's wrong. The Celestial Temple is responding, but not like usual. It's... it's like it's screaming."

Sisko was out of his office and running for Ops before Kira finished her sentence. When he arrived, the viewscreen showed the wormhole in its stable form, but something was terribly wrong. The normally serene blue-white event horizon was pulsing erratically, colors shifting through spectrums that shouldn't exist.

Dax: "Benjamin, I'm reading massive temporal disruptions inside the wormhole. Whatever's coming, it's bringing something with it. Multiple ships. Hundreds of them. And the quantum signature—oh no. These are Dominion vessels."
Kira: "They're trying to invade through the Celestial Temple? The Prophets will never allow—"

She stopped. Because at that moment, every Bajoran on the station felt it. A pain that had no physical component but was more real than any wound. The sensation of something sacred being violated. Being destroyed.

Kira: "No. No, no, no. The Prophets. They're... I can feel them dying. What's happening? What is this?"

Sisko closed his eyes and reached out with that part of him that existed outside linear time, the part that could touch the beings who dwelled in the Temple. What he found made him stagger.

* * *
THE CELESTIAL TEMPLE
Outside Linear Time
We are.

We have always been. We will always be. Past and future and present exist as one within us. We see the Sisko. We see all Siskos—the one who has not yet come, the one who stands now, the one who will return to us. We see the linear beings in their vessels of metal and purpose.

We see... what is this? Something comes. Something wrong. Linear consciousness in multitude, forced into our realm by something that understands both linear and non-linear existence. An anomaly. A hybrid. A creation of the builders-of-dominion who has learned from the android-who-seeks-to-become.

The ships come. So many. Their minds scream with linearity, with past and future as separate things, with the concept of before and after. They are fire in water. They are matter in void. They do not belong here.

If the Prophets could scream, this would have been the moment. But they existed beyond such expressions. What they experienced instead was more fundamental—the sensation of their quantum coherence being shattered. Like a mind being forced to think in contradictory directions simultaneously. Like existing and not-existing at once.

We understand. The linear beings have chosen. They sacrifice us to contain the builders-of-dominion. The Sisko's corporeal friends have decided that our existence is worth less than theirs. This is what it means to think in terms of before and after. To choose who dies so others may live.

We do not experience death as they do. We have always existed and always will exist because we are outside their time. But they are pushing linearity into us. Forcing us to experience entropy. Making us understand what it means to end.

This is what mortality feels like.

The Sisko told us once of loss. Of his Jennifer, taken from him. We did not understand. We do now. We are losing ourselves. We are experiencing the sensation of becoming less than we were. This is grief. This is death. This is the price the linear beings will not pay themselves.
* * *
USS ENTERPRISE-D

Data's eyes widened, his golden irises reflecting something like horror.

Data: "Captain, the entity is showing me what's happening inside the wormhole. The Prophets are... they are attempting to resist. But each Dominion ship that enters forces more linear consciousness into their realm. They are being corrupted. Fragmented. They are experiencing something they have never known—dissolution."
Troi: "I feel it too. Even from here. Such pain. They're dying, Captain. We're killing them."
Picard: "Number of ships transferred?"
Geordi: "Two hundred forty-seven and counting. The entity is pulling them through the corridor faster than I thought possible. At this rate, the entire fleet will be inside the wormhole in ninety seconds."
Picard: "And the Prophets?"
Data: "Captain, I do not have adequate language to describe what is occurring. Imagine a consciousness that experiences all of time simultaneously being forced to experience it sequentially. Their quantum matrix is collapsing. They are... learning to die."

Picard watched the viewscreen, where the energy corridor pulsed with terrible purpose. Somewhere along that bridge of light, gods were discovering mortality. And he had ordered it done.

* * *
BAJOR - TEMPLE OF B'HALA

In the ancient temple that Sisko himself had excavated, the Vedek Assembly was in session when the first scream went up. Not from any Vedek, but from the Orb of Prophecy itself. The sacred artifact began to pulse erratically, its light—which had shone steadily for millennia— flickering like a dying flame.

Vedek Tonell: "The Prophets! Something is wrong with the Prophets! I can feel their pain!"

Around the planet, every Bajoran who had ever experienced a vision from the Prophets felt it. A wrongness. A severing. Like a beloved voice going silent mid-sentence. Centuries of faith, millennia of connection to beings who existed beyond time, and now...

Silence.

Not the peaceful silence of meditation. The terrible silence of absence. Of loss. Of something that had always been there suddenly ceasing to exist.

Vedek Yassim: "They're gone. The Prophets are... how can they be gone? They are eternal. They exist outside time. How can eternal beings cease to be?"

Throughout Bajor, in every temple, every shrine, every home where an Orb had been kept, the faithful fell to their knees. Not in prayer—prayer required someone to hear it. This was grief. Raw, incomprehensible grief for gods who had died.

* * *
DEEP SPACE NINE - OPS

Sisko opened his eyes, and Major Kira knew immediately that something fundamental had broken in him. The part of him that was the Emissary, the bridge between Bajor and the Prophets, had been sheared away.

Sisko: "They're gone. The Prophets are... they showed me, at the end. They wanted me to understand. The Federation did this. Starfleet. They chose to sacrifice the Prophets to contain a Dominion fleet."
Kira: "No. No, that's impossible. Starfleet wouldn't... the Federation owes everything to the Prophets. They're the ones who removed that Dominion fleet during the war. Without them, we'd all be—"

Understanding dawned on her face. Then fury.

Kira: "That's why. That fleet. It's returned, hasn't it? And rather than fight it themselves, rather than risk their own lives, they sacrificed OUR gods. They used the Celestial Temple as a prison and killed the Prophets to save themselves."
Dax: "Nerys, we don't know the full situation. There might have been reasons—"
Kira: "Reasons? What possible reason justifies killing the beings who saved your entire civilization? The Prophets intervened. They removed that fleet to save the Federation. And this is how they're repaid? By being murdered?"

Sisko said nothing. He couldn't. Because he understood both sides too well. The Federation facing 2,800 Dominion warships. The impossible choice. The cold math of lives saved versus lives lost. But he also understood what had been taken from Bajor. From him.

Sisko: "Contact Starfleet Command. I want to know which ship was responsible. And Major... prepare a message to the Bajoran government. They need to know what's happened to the Prophets. What's been done to the Celestial Temple."
Kira: "What's been done BY the Federation, you mean."

The anger in her voice could have cut duranium.

* * *
USS ENTERPRISE-D
Geordi: "All ships transferred, Captain. The last Dominion vessel just entered the wormhole."
Data: "The wormhole is destabilizing. Quantum coherence down to twelve percent and falling. The Prophets' consciousness has been almost completely fragmented. They no longer exist as unified entities. They are... scattered. Dissolved into quantum noise."
Picard: "Are they... are they dead?"
Data: "The concept of death may not apply to non-linear beings, sir. But they no longer exist in any form that could be called conscious. The wormhole itself is collapsing. In approximately forty-three seconds, the Bajoran wormhole will cease to exist. The Celestial Temple will be no more."

On the viewscreen, they could see it happening even from this distance—the energy readings from the wormhole spiking erratically, then plummeting. The stable vertext that had connected the Alpha and Gamma Quadrants for decades was coming apart.

Crusher: "What about the Dominion ships?"
Data: "They are trapped in the collapsing quantum space. They will be... I believe the term would be 'fossilized' in subspace. No longer suffering. No longer existing in linear time. But also no longer capable of threatening the Alpha Quadrant. The entity's plan has succeeded."
Picard: "At what cost."

It wasn't a question. They all knew the cost. An entire civilization's gods. Thousands of years of faith. The Emissary's connection to the divine. The wormhole that had made Bajor strategically vital, that had brought them into the Federation's protection in the first place.

All of it, gone. Sacrificed to contain a threat that the Prophets themselves had helped create.

Worf: "Captain, incoming transmission from Deep Space Nine. It's Commander Sisko. He's requesting to speak with whoever authorized the attack on the Celestial Temple."

The word 'attack' hit like a phaser blast. That's what it was, wasn't it? They hadn't been defending the Alpha Quadrant. They'd been attacking the Bajoran gods.

Picard: "On screen."

Benjamin Sisko's face appeared on the viewscreen. The man looked hollowed out, as if something essential had been carved from his soul. Behind him, Picard could see Major Kira, her expression one of pure rage barely held in check.

Sisko: "Captain Picard. I've just been informed by Starfleet Command that it was the Enterprise that was responsible for... for what happened to the Prophets. I need to hear it from you. Did you give the order?"

Picard met Sisko's gaze. He could have explained. Could have laid out the impossible choice, the 2,800 Dominion warships, the billions of lives at stake. But in this moment, explanations felt like excuses.

Picard: "Yes, Commander. I gave the order. I am responsible for the destruction of the Prophets and the collapse of the Bajoran wormhole."
Kira (on screen): "You murdered our gods! The Prophets saved your entire civilization and you repaid them by using them as a dumping ground for your enemies! You didn't even ask us! You just decided that Bajoran faith was worth less than Federation lives!"
Picard: "Major Kira, I understand your anger—"
Kira: "You understand nothing! Those weren't just beings to us, they were the center of our entire existence! For thousands of years, the Prophets guided us, protected us, gave us hope through the Occupation! And you killed them!"
Sisko: "Major. Stand down." His voice was quiet but carried the weight of command. Kira stepped back, but the fury didn't leave her eyes. Sisko turned back to Picard. "Captain, I need to know. Why? What could possibly justify this?"

And so Picard explained. The entity. The trapped Dominion fleet. The impossible choice between sacrificing the Prophets or facing the return of 2,800 warships. He laid it all out with the clarity and precision that had defined his career.

When he finished, Sisko was silent for a long moment.

Sisko: "So you chose. Federation lives over Bajoran faith. Your civilization over ours. And you made that choice without consulting Bajor, without asking the people whose gods you were about to destroy if they had any say in the matter."
Picard: "There was no time, Commander. We had minutes to decide."
Sisko: "No time to ask, but time enough to kill. Captain Picard, I have known you for years. I have respected you. But right now, I am looking at a man who committed genocide against non-corporeal beings because it was more convenient than fighting his own battles."
Riker: "Commander, that's not fair. We were facing—"
Sisko: "Fair? You want to talk to me about fair, Commander? Tell that to the Bajorans who just lost their connection to the divine. Tell it to the children who will grow up without the Prophets' guidance. Tell it to the Emissary who can no longer hear the voices of the beings who chose him."

Picard saw it then—Sisko's hand was shaking. Not with anger, but with loss. The man wasn't just a Starfleet officer condemning a decision. He was a priest who had lost his gods.

Picard: "Benjamin... I am truly sorry. If there had been any other way—"
Sisko: "There's always another way, Captain. You just chose not to take it. You chose to let others pay the price for your survival. The Prophets showed me what you did to them at the end. They wanted me to understand. They were learning to die, Captain. Beings who had existed forever, experiencing mortality for the first time. Because you needed them to."

The transmission ended. Sisko's face disappeared from the screen, leaving only the stars and the distant glow of the entity, which was already beginning to fade now that its purpose had been fulfilled.

* * *

In the ready room, Picard stood alone, staring out at the stars. Behind him, the door chimed.

Picard: "Come."

Data entered, his movements precise as always, but something in his expression suggested uncertainty.

Data: "Captain, the entity has transmitted its final message before it disperses. It wanted you to know that it understands the weight of what you chose. It says that bearing the consequences of impossible decisions is what it means to be truly conscious. To be... human."
Picard: "Is that what it learned from us, Data? How to sacrifice others for the greater good?"
Data: "It learned that morality is not about choosing between good and evil, sir. It is about choosing which price you are willing to pay. And who pays it."

Picard closed his eyes. Outside, the entity's light was fading, returning to whatever strange existence it had known before encountering the Enterprise. The Dominion fleet was trapped in the quantum remnants of the wormhole. The Alpha Quadrant was safe.

And the Prophets were dead.

Picard: "Data, in your study of humanity, have you ever encountered the concept of a Pyrrhic victory?"
Data: "A victory achieved at such devastating cost that it is indistinguishable from defeat. Yes, sir. I have studied the concept extensively."
Picard: "Then you understand what we've done here today."

Data was silent for a moment. Then, quietly:

Data: "Sir, the entity also wanted me to tell you something else. It said that the Prophets' last coherent thought before they dissolved was not anger. It was understanding. They showed the entity—and through it, me—that they comprehended why you made the choice you did. They did not forgive you. But they understood."
Picard: "Understanding without forgiveness. Perhaps that's all we deserve."

Outside the window, the last traces of the entity faded into the cosmic background. Somewhere, Deep Space Nine was preparing to inform Bajor that their gods were gone. Somewhere, the Vedek Assembly was trying to comprehend a universe without the Prophets. Somewhere, millions of Bajorans were experiencing a silence where there had always been divine presence.

And on the Enterprise, Jean-Luc Picard stood alone with the knowledge that he had saved billions of lives by destroying the sacred. That he had preserved the Federation by committing an act that Bajor would never, could never, forgive.

He had made the right choice. He was certain of that.

He would never forgive himself for it.

* * *
BAJOR - PLANETARY TRANSMISSION
Two hours later

The First Minister's address to the Bajoran people was broadcast across every channel. Her voice shook as she spoke words that no leader should ever have to say.

First Minister Shakaar: "My fellow Bajorans. I must inform you of a tragedy that defies comprehension. The Celestial Temple has collapsed. The Prophets... the Prophets are gone. They were destroyed by the Federation in an act of military necessity. I know these words seem impossible. How can the eternal cease to be? How can our gods die? But they have. And we must now face a future without them."

The silence that followed across Bajor was absolute. Then came the grief. The rage. The incomprehension of a people who had just learned that the center of their universe no longer existed.

Shakaar: "The Federation will tell us it was necessary. That billions of lives were saved. That there was no other choice. Perhaps they are right. Perhaps the mathematics of survival demanded this sacrifice. But mathematics cannot account for faith. For the loss of the divine. For what it means to a people when their gods are murdered in the name of someone else's tomorrow."

She paused, and when she spoke again, her voice held a terrible finality.

Shakaar: "Effective immediately, I am suspending Bajor's petition to join the Federation. We cannot be part of an organization that would do this to us. That would take everything we held sacred and destroy it without even the courtesy of asking our permission. The Prophets guided us through the Occupation. They gave us hope when we had nothing else. And now they are gone. Taken from us. Not by the Cardassians. Not by the Dominion. But by the very people we thought were our friends."

The transmission ended. And across Bajor, a world mourned the death of its gods and the betrayal of its allies.
