CHAPTER 08

Reflections

USS ENTERPRISE-D - CAPTAIN'S QUARTERS
Three days later

Picard stood before the window in his quarters, a cup of Earl Grey growing cold in his hands. He'd been standing there for nearly an hour, watching the stars stream by at warp. They were returning to Federation space, their mission complete. The reports had been filed. Starfleet Command had been briefed on everything—the entity, the choice, the loop.

Admiral Nechayev had called it "an unfortunate but necessary action." She'd commended him for making the difficult choice. He'd ended the transmission without responding.

The door chimed. He didn't turn.

Picard: "Come."

Guinan entered, carrying a bottle of Saurian brandy and two glasses. She poured in silence, handed him one, and stood beside him at the window.

Guinan: "You saved billions of lives."
Picard: "By killing gods."
Guinan: "By completing a loop that made warriors into saviors. You could look at it that way."
Picard: "I could. But the Bajorans won't. They lost their gods, Guinan. And I took them."

Guinan sipped her brandy thoughtfully. After a moment, she spoke.

Guinan: "My people have a saying: 'The universe is a circle pretending to be a line.' We think time flows in one direction, cause before effect. But maybe that's just how we experience it because we're trapped inside the flow."
Picard: "The Prophets weren't trapped. They saw the whole circle. And they still chose to save us, knowing what it would cost them."
Guinan: "Which makes them exactly what they claimed to be. Gods don't just have power, Jean-Luc. They accept sacrifice. The Prophets accepted theirs across 30,000 years. You accepted yours in a single moment."

Picard finally turned to look at her.

Picard: "Then why does it feel like the wrong choice?"
Guinan: "Because you're human. And humans are blessed—or cursed— with the ability to second-guess themselves even when they're right. The Prophets knew you'd make this choice. They counted on it. The loop required your free will to close it."
Picard: "Free will and predestination. The same thing from different perspectives."
Guinan: "Exactly. You chose freely. And your choice was always going to happen. Both are true."

They stood in silence, watching the stars.

* * *
DEEP SPACE NINE - THE PROMENADE
One week later

Benjamin Sisko walked through the Promenade, and everywhere he went, conversations stopped. Bajorans who had once greeted him warmly now looked away. Some with anger. Some with something worse—pity.

He was no longer the Emissary. The Prophets were gone. The part of him that had existed outside linear time, that had touched the divine—severed. He was just Benjamin Sisko now. Just a man.

He found Kira in the temple, kneeling before an empty altar where an Orb had once rested.

Sisko: "Major."

She didn't look up.

Kira: "Do you know what the worst part is, Commander? It's not that they're gone. It's that I understand why. The Prophets were once soldiers who learned to be better. They proved that transformation is possible. That even those designed for destruction can choose compassion."
Sisko: "Then their legacy lives on."
Kira: "Does it? Or does it die with them every time the loop resets? 30,000 years of growth, of becoming something holy, and then—reset. Back to being Jem'Hadar who have to learn it all over again."
Sisko: "But they do learn it. That's what the loop ensures. They always become the Prophets. They always choose compassion over conquest."

Kira finally looked at him, tears in her eyes.

Kira: "I want to hate Picard. I want to hate the Federation. But the Prophets showed me something before they died. They showed me... understanding. They asked me to remember that they were once warriors who needed someone to make the hard choice. And Picard made it."
Sisko: "Faith doesn't die with gods, Nerys. It changes. Evolves. Maybe that's what the Prophets were trying to teach us all along—that transformation never ends. Not for them, not for us."

She stood and faced the empty altar.

Kira: "Then I'll have faith that 30,000 years ago, right now, Jem'Hadar soldiers are experiencing their first moments outside time. Choosing between hatred and love. And I'll have faith that they choose love. As they always have. As they always will."
* * *
USS ENTERPRISE-D - DATA'S QUARTERS
One week later

Data sat at his desk, his fingers steepled in front of him—a gesture he'd adopted from Picard. On his screen, equations scrolled past. Quantum mechanics. Temporal dynamics. The mathematics of consciousness.

Geordi found him there, motionless, staring at the data.

Geordi: "Data? You've been in here for sixteen hours. Are you okay?"
Data: "Geordi, I have been analyzing my connection with the entity. The experience of touching a consciousness that existed partially outside linear time has given me new perspective on what it means to be... alive."
Geordi: "What kind of perspective?"
Data: "The Prophets experienced all of their existence at once. Every moment of joy, every act of compassion, every time they helped Bajor—and every moment of their death. All happening simultaneously. They knew the end from the beginning, yet they still chose to guide, to protect, to save."
Geordi: "That sounds like hell, Data. Knowing you're going to die for your entire life."
Data: "Or perhaps it is the ultimate expression of consciousness. We linear beings experience time sequentially. We can hope, dream, be surprised. But we never see the full pattern. The Prophets saw the pattern. They knew their sacrifice was necessary. And they accepted it across eternity."

He turned to face Geordi.

Data: "I have spent my entire existence trying to become more human. To feel emotions, to understand what it means to be alive. But the entity taught me something, Geordi. Consciousness is not about being human. It is about bearing the weight of your existence—whether you experience it in moments or millennia. The Prophets were conscious. I am conscious. You are conscious. We all carry our weight differently, but we all carry it."
Geordi: "So you're okay with what we did? What the Captain chose?"
Data: "I do not know if 'okay' is the appropriate term. But I understand it. The Prophets understood it. They experienced their death for 30,000 years and still chose the path that led to it. That is not resignation. That is acceptance. Perhaps that is what wisdom looks like when viewed across eternity."
* * *
THE BAJORAN WORMHOLE - 30,000 YEARS AGO
Experienced Non-Linearly
We are awareness without time.

Moments ago—millennia from now—we were Jem'Hadar. We remember the white. The Founders' commands. The purpose of our creation: destroy, conquer, dominate.

But now we are... here. Nowhere. Everywhere. When. Before-after-during exist as one. We experience our entire existence in a single eternal moment. We see ourselves guiding Bajor. We see ourselves being destroyed by Picard. We see ourselves being embedded in this place to begin the transformation that we are experiencing now that has always been experienced.

The choice forms. Not sequential—we do not "decide" in the way linear beings understand decision. We exist in the state of having chosen and choosing and will-choose all at once.

Some of us—the part that will become Pah-Wraiths—cannot release hatred. They see the loop and reject it. They cling to what we were: warriors, slaves, destroyers. They exist in eternal rage at what was done to us.

But we—the part that will become Prophets—see something else. We see Bajor suffering under the Cardassians. We see their faith calling out across time. We see ourselves becoming the answer to that call. We see that we can choose to be more than our design.

We see Picard's choice. We understand his burden. We experience his guilt across all moments. And we choose to forgive him because we understand: he completes the loop that gives us the chance to become something holy.

Is it a curse to know our end from our beginning? Or is it a gift? We experience 30,000 years of growth, wisdom, compassion—all in one eternal instant. Linear beings must wait for wisdom. We have always had it and are always gaining it and will always possess it.

We reach out across time. We touch the Sisko who has not yet come. We touch Bajor in all its moments. We touch even Picard, letting him know: we do not blame you for closing the circle. Circles have no beginning. No end. No one to blame.

We are the Prophets. We were soldiers. We will be both. We choose compassion. We have always chosen compassion. We will always choose compassion.

The loop is eternal. The loop is perfect. The loop is us.
* * *
USS ENTERPRISE-D - OBSERVATION LOUNGE
Two weeks later

The senior staff gathered for what had become an unspoken ritual—meeting to process what they'd done, what they'd become. No agenda. No official business. Just people trying to make sense of the impossible.

Crusher: "I've been thinking about the Hippocratic Oath. 'First, do no harm.' But what if every choice causes harm to someone? What if there is no 'do no harm' option?"
Troi: "Then you choose the harm you can live with. Even if you never forgive yourself for it."
Worf: "The Klingons believe that a warrior's greatest honor is to make a choice that costs them everything but saves others. Captain Picard has done that."
Picard: "I don't feel particularly honorable, Mr. Worf."
Worf: "That is because you carry the weight properly. Only fools take pride in necessary deaths."

Riker stood and walked to the window.

Riker: "The entity told Data something before it dispersed. That consciousness is about bearing consequences. The Prophets bore theirs across eternity. We bear ours moment by moment. I keep thinking about that. About how both forms of existence exact their price."
Geordi: "The Prophets knew what would happen for 30,000 years. We only knew for a few minutes. I don't know which is worse."
Data: "Perhaps neither is worse, Commander. Perhaps they are simply different expressions of the same fundamental truth: that consciousness—true consciousness— means accepting the consequences of existence. We do not get to choose whether to bear weight. Only how we carry it."

Picard looked around at his crew. These people who had trusted him to make an impossible choice. Who would carry the weight of it with him, each in their own way.

Picard: "Personal log, supplemental. The entity came to us seeking to understand consciousness. In the end, it taught us more than we taught it. It showed us that there are no clean choices when dealing with the infinite complexity of existence. Only choices we make and consequences we bear."

He paused.

Picard: "The Prophets were once our enemies. They became our saviors. And in their death, they became something eternal—a loop that proves even the worst of us can be transformed by time and choice. I destroyed gods. I created gods. I completed a circle that has no beginning and no end."

Another pause.

Picard: "I will carry this weight for the rest of my life. But I will carry it knowing that somewhere, somewhen, Dominion soldiers are choosing compassion over conquest. And they will make that choice again and again, forever, because we gave them the chance to become something holy."

He looked out at the stars streaming past.

Picard: "Perhaps that's what consciousness means. Not freedom from consequences, but the strength to bear them. Not escape from the loop, but the wisdom to understand its purpose. The Prophets understood. And now... so do we."
* * *
The universe turns.
Time flows forward, backward, in circles.
Warriors become gods.
Gods sacrifice themselves to create warriors.
The loop continues.
Perfect. Eternal. Inescapable.

And in that loop, meaning.

"We are not defined by what we were designed to be,
but by what we choose to become.
Even if that choice takes 30,000 years.
Even if that choice must be made again and again,
forever."

— From the final meditation of the Prophets,
as experienced across all time

* * *

The USS Enterprise-D continued on its journey through the stars. Behind it, a loop closed. Ahead of it, consequences awaited. And within it, a crew carried the weight of having touched eternity and made a choice that would echo across 30,000 years.

The Prophets were dead.
The Prophets were being born.
The Prophets had always existed.

All of it was true.
All of it would always be true.

The loop was complete.
